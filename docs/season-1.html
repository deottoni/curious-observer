Season 1

Jun — Nov 2026

I've been reading about psychology and human behavior for years — casually, inconsistently, the way most people engage with ideas they find interesting but never fully pursue. Good books, good podcasts, things that made me think. Never deeper than that.

Season 1 is the attempt to go deeper — and to do it seriously, with AI as a genuine research partner. There are two questions running in parallel. The first is about psychology: do I actually enjoy the methodology — reading papers, auditing methods, sitting with evidence — or just the high-level ideas? That's a real question, and six months of structured work should give me an honest answer. The result could mean a Master's application. Or it confirms this stays a rewarding intellectual hobby. Either outcome is fine; I just want to know. The second question is about AI: can a well-designed multi-agent system turn a curious non-academic into someone capable of engaging seriously with research literature? Season 1 is where both get tested.

The Curious Observer is bigger than this experiment. I'm naturally curious — there will be more to explore after November whether or not this ends with a grad school decision. But Season 1 has a specific shape: six books, twelve posts, one honest answer.

      The Moral Mind

      The Righteous Mind — Jonathan Haidt

      Why people have different moral intuitions. Why reasoning follows feeling rather than leading it. How tribalism is a feature — not a bug — of human psychology, and what the six moral foundations actually explain about why public arguments go nowhere.

      Papers Haidt (2001), "The emotional dog and its rational tail" · Graham, Haidt & Nosek (2009), "Liberals and conservatives rely on different sets of moral foundations"

      Skill How to read an academic paper. Abstract → Introduction → Discussion → Methods. Write one sentence per paper: what did they measure, and what did they find?

      Jul 2026 · Posts 3–4

      The Biology of Behavior

      Behave — Robert Sapolsky

      How hormones, genes, evolution, and neurobiology shape behavior. The biology of sex differences. What happens in the brain one second before a behavior — and one million years before it. The paternal biology post: what testosterone, oxytocin, and rough-and-tumble play tell us about fathers.

      Papers Buss (1995), "Evolutionary psychology: A new paradigm" · Feldman et al. (2010), "Natural variations in maternal and paternal care are associated with oxytocin"

      Skill Confounding variables. Testosterone correlates with aggression — but does it cause it? Practice asking: what else could explain this result?

      Aug 2026 · Posts 5–6

      How We Actually Think

      Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman

      System 1 and System 2. Heuristics and biases. Prospect theory and loss aversion. Framing effects. The ways humans are systematically irrational — and why. How cognitive biases shape parenting and early financial education.

      Papers Kahneman & Tversky (1979), "Prospect theory: An analysis of decision under risk" · Tversky & Kahneman (1974), "Judgment under uncertainty: Heuristics and biases"

      Skill Operationalization. How did they turn an abstract concept into a measurable, reproducible task? Could you design a different experiment that tests the same hypothesis?

      Sep 2026 · Posts 7–8

      Culture's Hidden Architecture

      The WEIRDest People in the World — Joseph Henrich

      How WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) populations are statistical outliers in human history. How culture shapes cognition. How the nuclear family is historically novel — and how the fatherlessness crisis is partly a WEIRD-society crisis.

      Papers Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan (2010), "The weirdest people in the world" · Putnam (2000), "Bowling alone: America's declining social capital"

      Skill Population validity. Who is in this sample? Can these findings travel to other populations, cultures, or time periods?

      Oct 2026 · Posts 9–10

      Reading Science Honestly

      The Elephant in the Brain — Robin Hanson & Kevin Simler

      Hidden motives in human behavior. How scientific institutions produce biased findings. The replication crisis, p-hacking, and publication bias. How to evaluate evidence quality — not just evidence existence. Minimum: one paper per week this month.

      Papers Ioannidis (2005), "Why most published research findings are false" · Open Science Collaboration (2015), "Estimating the reproducibility of psychological science"

      Skill Meta-analysis and publication bias detection. Stop evaluating single studies; start evaluating what the field as a whole says.

      Nov 2026 · Posts 11–12

      Where Do I Stand?

      Moral Tribes — Joshua Greene

      Applying five months of tools to a synthesis question. Identifying the subfield where curiosity is sharpest. Post 12 is the honest essay: first-person, no summary — a real answer to the question this whole experiment was built around. What did the methodology feel like? What surprised? What do I want more of?

      Papers 2–3 papers in whichever subfield is pulling hardest by October. Self-selected based on what the reading reveals — not pre-assigned.

      Skill Identifying the gap. Every research paper ends with "future directions." A thesis lives in those questions. By Post 12: read a paper, find what it leaves unanswered, and say why it matters.

      The decision Master's track or lifelong hobby. Both are valid. The project tells the answer; you just have to read it honestly.
