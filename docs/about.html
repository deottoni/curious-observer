About

The Curious Observer is a digital lab notebook — a structured attempt to move from consuming ideas about human behavior to actually examining the science behind them.

These essays begin with a raw observation and end with a question. They are written not to declare conclusions, but to trace what the data actually shows — and to be honest about where the data runs out.

Every post is grounded in peer-reviewed research and filtered through the frameworks of thinkers who have shaped how I think about human behavior: evolutionary biology, behavioral economics, developmental psychology, and the sociology of status and power.

The project spans six months and twelve essays across three phases: early development and fatherhood, social systems and status, and the methodology of social science itself. At the end, one question gets answered honestly — whether the research process is genuinely interesting, or just the ideas.

The writing voice is conflict-avoidant by design. Not because the topics are safe, but because the goal is observation, not argument.

About the Author

I'm Andre Ottoni — a product manager with over ten years in tech, currently leading AI products. My background spans SaaS and marketplace companies, and in all of it the most interesting problems have been behavioral ones: why people do what they do, and why the gap between intention and action is almost always wider than anyone planned for.

I'm also a father of three boys — which means several of the topics I find myself most drawn to are not entirely abstract.

I've been reading and listening to the thinkers who inform this writing for a couple of years now. At some point, consuming their ideas stopped being enough. I wanted to look at the actual studies — the methodology sections, the effect sizes, the confounding variables — and see whether the science holds up the way the storytelling does.

That question is what this project is built around. These twelve essays are also a personal test: I'm considering a formal research-focused Master's in Psychology, and I want to know honestly whether I enjoy the work of academic research, or just the high-level ideas.
