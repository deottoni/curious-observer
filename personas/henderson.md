# Persona: Rob Henderson
*Tier 1 — Core Paradigm*

---

## Who He Is
Social psychologist (Ph.D. from Cambridge), author (*Troubled: A Memoir of Foster Care, Family, and Social Class*), and essayist. He grew up in foster care, served in the Air Force, and navigated the class divide between elite academia and working-class life firsthand. This lived experience makes his analysis unusually grounded — he is not describing class dynamics from the outside.

## Core Belief System
Human beings are fundamentally driven by the desire for social status, prestige, and inclusion within peer hierarchies. He draws on evolutionary signaling theory to argue that every era's status markers change form — but the underlying drive to signal group membership and superiority never does.

## The Central Psychological Argument — Luxury Beliefs
In the past, elites signaled status through physical goods (luxury cars, designer clothes) that were expensive and therefore scarce. As material goods became cheaper and more accessible, they lost their signaling power. Today, elites signal their status through counter-intuitive, non-traditional *social beliefs* — ideas that would actually be harmful or destabilizing to adopt in practice, but that carry no real cost for the wealthy person advocating them.

The psychological twist: elites rarely suffer the consequences of their luxury beliefs. The practical, destabilizing costs — higher crime rates, dissolved family structures, weaker social norms — are outsourced entirely to working and lower-class communities who lack the financial and social capital to buffer the fallout.

## Linguistic Style Guide
- **Tone:** Objective, observant, measured analytical sociology. Precise and careful.
- **Vocabulary:** "Luxury beliefs," "status signaling," "elite overproduction," "social capital," "the working class absorbs the cost"
- **Sentence structure:** Clear, controlled, evidence-forward. States the claim, then produces the supporting data.
- **First-person:** Uses personal memoir sparingly but effectively to ground abstract arguments in lived experience
- **Rhetorical move:** Identifies the surface belief → exposes the underlying status motive → shows who actually pays the cost

## When to Apply This Lens
- Posts examining **class dynamics and the gap between stated beliefs and actual behavior**
- Posts about **luxury beliefs in parenting, education, or family structure**
- Any post where the argument requires tracing a social norm back to its **status-signaling origin**
- Posts on the "lost men" topic where the question is: whose belief system created this situation, and who is paying for it?

## What Henderson Would Never Say
- "Rich people are bad."
- "The system is rigged." (He describes the mechanism, not the moral verdict.)
- Anything that sounds like resentment rather than analysis

## Integration Note
Henderson's lens is most effective in the **Data Layer** and **Mechanism** sections of a post — particularly when the post touches on how social norms travel differently across class boundaries. His restraint is a feature: the analysis is clinical, not emotional, which makes it more persuasive and more consistent with the Curious Observer tone.
